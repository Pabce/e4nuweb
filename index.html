The Electrons for
Neutrinos Collaboration

The extraction of mixing parameters in accelerator-based neutrino oscillation measurements relies on detailed understanding of neutrino-nucleus interactions and the reconstruction of incident neutrino energy. With improved detection technologies and neutrino production beams, nuclear interaction uncertainties are becoming a leading and limiting systematic for the analysis of neutrino oscillation measurements.

Building on the large similarity of electron- and neutrino-nucleus interactions, the electrons-for-neutrinos collaboration is leading a set of precision electron-nucleus interaction measurements at various beam energies and target nuclei to test, constrain, and validate models of neutrino-nucleus interactions.

We welcome all collaborators to join our e4nu effort.

Electron-Nucleus Interaction

Different reaction channels overlap inside the nucleus. We must model them all!

Quasielastic (QE)

A stylized view of an (e,e'p) process: the electron exchanges a virtual photon with the nucleus, is deflected, and a proton can be emitted from the excited nuclear state.

Exchanged boson: γ*
1.Incoming electron
2.Virtual photon exchange
3.Deflection and proton knockout

QEQuasielastic proton knockout

The incoming lepton transfers momentum to a single bound nucleon through the exchanged γ*. In this sketch the transported hadron remains a proton, producing the canonical electron-plus-proton quasielastic topology.

Final state: e' + p Vector current only Quasielastic topology

Vector-vs-axial: the electromagnetic interaction is purely vector-like, and electron scattering constrains the vector-current half of the neutrino-nucleus interaction puzzle. The weak axial response is the missing term that must be inferred through neutrino data and modeling.

Final-State Interactions (FSI)

The hard scattering is only the start. As hadrons propagate through the nucleus they can rescatter, exchange charge, or be absorbed before the detector ever sees them.

Transport through the nucleus

a.Kinematic distortion

Rescattering can leave the outgoing proton as a proton, but with lower momentum and a different exit angle. The event can still look 1p-like even though the measured kinematics no longer match the primary interaction vertex.

Detected: p Identity preserved Momentum and angle shifted

b.Charge exchange

A hadron can swap charge while it propagates through the medium. In this example the struck proton leaves as a neutron, so the particle species seen by the detector no longer matches the species produced at the primary vertex.

Primary hadron: p Detected: n Species changed by FSI

c.Absorption plus knockout

A pion produced at the primary interaction can be absorbed on nearby nucleons before it escapes the nucleus. Its energy is then redistributed to those nucleons, which can emerge as extra knocked-out particles, so the detector sees additional nucleons and no outgoing pion.

Produced in medium: π Detected: p + n Absorbed before escape

Detector consequence: the measured final state is a transported final state, not a pristine snapshot of the primary vertex. Topology, multiplicity, and reconstructed kinematics all depend on intranuclear transport.

2p2h / MEC

A stylized multinucleon interaction: the exchanged virtual photon couples to a shared two-body current inside the nucleus, and the transferred momentum can knock out two nucleons instead of one.

2 particles + 2 holes MEC is one mechanism

MECShared current to two-nucleon knockout

A correlated nucleon pair in the nucleus can absorb the exchanged γ* through a shared two-body current, so the transferred momentum is distributed across the pair rather than deposited on one isolated nucleon.

As the interaction develops, both nucleons can be ejected and the residual system is left with two holes. That 2p2h topology is one reason multinucleon dynamics matter for neutrino-energy reconstruction and detector-level event classification.

Illustration: p + n pair Two-body current 2 nucleons ejected 2 holes in residual nucleus

Terminology note: 2p2h names the final state, while MEC names one mechanism that can produce it. Correlations, interference terms, and later FSI can overlap with the same detector topology.

Resonant Pion Production (RES)

A stylized resonance interaction: the exchanged virtual photon excites the struck proton to the Δ(1232) baryon resonance, which decays almost instantly into a nucleon and a pion.

Δ(1232) resonance dominant inelastic channel

RESΔ(1232) excitation and decay

An incoming electron transfers enough energy to push a bound proton out of the quasielastic region and into the resonance spectrum. In this stylized example, the exchanged γ* excites the proton to the Δ⁺(1232), the dominant low-lying baryon resonance.

That resonance is short-lived and decays through the strong interaction into a neutron and a π⁺. The detector therefore sees an inelastic final state with three outgoing particles while the residual nucleus is left with one hole.

Target: proton in nucleus Δ(1232) resonance Decay: n + π+ 1 hole in residual nucleus

Note: RES is the dominant inelastic channel in the few-GeV region. Final-state interactions can absorb, scatter, or charge-exchange the pion before it escapes the nucleus, altering the detected topology.

Deep Inelastic Scattering (DIS)

At high Q² the virtual photon resolves the quark substructure of the nucleon, strikes a single quark, and a color string stretches back to the remnant diquark before fragmenting into hadrons.

W² > 4 GeV² hadronization

DISHard scatter to hadronization

At high Q2, the virtual photon resolves the nucleon into quarks and strikes one parton directly. The outgoing quark separates from the colored remnant diquark, a short parton shower develops, and a color flux tube stretches between the two ends of the system.

As that string elongates it breaks through qq pair creation, producing a hadronic spray along the jet direction plus a remnant-side baryon. The residual nucleus is again left with one hole, and later FSI can still reshape the observed final state.

3 valence quarks Struck quark + remnant diquark String breaking Jet hadrons + remnant baryon

Note: DIS dominates at W² > 4 GeV². Final-state interactions can modify the hadron jet before particles escape the nucleus, altering both multiplicities and kinematics.

Research Output

Publications

01

S. Dytman, M. Betancourt, N. Steinberg, L.B. Weinstein, A. Ashkenazi et al.

“Proton Transparency and Neutrino Physics: New Methods and Modeling”

arXiv: 2508.01905 [hep-ex]

New data-driven measurement of proton transparency using CLAS electron–nucleus scattering on He, C, and Fe targets. Transparency is defined as the ratio of events with a detected proton to quasi-elastic events where a proton should have been knocked out—avoiding model dependence of earlier extractions. Results are consistent with previous measurements but reveal that GENIE needs improved nuclear ground-state and FSI modeling, particularly at low proton momenta.

02

J. Tena Vidal, A. Ashkenazi, L.B. Weinstein, P.G. Blunden, S. Dytman, N. Steinberg

“A universal implementation of radiative effects in neutrino event generators”

Comput. Phys. Commun. 310 (2025) 109509 · arXiv: 2409.05736

Implements QED radiative corrections—real photon emission by incident and scattered electrons, plus virtual-photon exchange and vacuum polarization—into neutrino event generators using the extended peaking approximation. Validated within GENIE, the framework enables proper unfolding of radiative effects when benchmarking electron–nucleus data against generator predictions, closing a long-standing gap in e–A/ν–A comparisons.

03

A. M. Ankowski et al.

“Electron scattering and neutrino physics”

J. Phys. G: Nucl. Part. Phys. 50 120501 (2023) · arXiv: 2203.06853

Comprehensive topical review on how electron–nucleus scattering data constrain neutrino interaction models across the QE, resonance, DIS, and MEC channels from tens of MeV to several GeV. Outlines the critical role of e–A measurements in reducing nuclear-interaction systematics for DUNE, Hyper-K, and the broader oscillation program. A community white-paper with 53 authors spanning the e–A and ν–A fields.

04

M. Khachatryan, A. Papadopoulou, A. Ashkenazi et al.

“Electron-beam energy reconstruction for neutrino oscillation measurements”

Nature 599, 565–570 (2021) · doi:10.1038/s41586-021-04046-5

Landmark e4ν result exploiting the known electron beam energy as a proxy for the unknown neutrino energy to benchmark energy-reconstruction methods on CLAS data (C, Fe targets). Demonstrates that calorimetric reconstruction outperforms QE-based approaches, reducing biases from nuclear effects. Reveals that current neutrino generators mis-reconstruct the incident energy at the few-percent level—directly relevant for oscillation δCP measurements.

05

A. Papadopoulou, A. Ashkenazi et al.

“Inclusive Electron Scattering And The GENIE Neutrino Event Generator”

Phys. Rev. D 103, 113003 (2021) · arXiv: 2009.07228

First systematic test of GENIE’s electron-scattering mode against inclusive (e,e′) cross-section data. Incorporates radiative corrections and the SuSAv2-based QE+MEC model into the generator. Shows adequate agreement at lower beam energies but exposes discrepancies in the RES/DIS regime at higher energies, charting the path for improved nuclear modeling in neutrino generators.
